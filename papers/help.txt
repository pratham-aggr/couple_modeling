--- Updated data-split paragraph (temporal / CAMulator split) ---

Samples are partitioned chronologically rather than randomly, following the
CAMulator protocol (Chapman et al., 2025), so that evaluation measures genuine
out-of-sample generalization to future years rather than interpolation within
the training period. The first 31 years (1980--2010) are used for training,
2011--2012 for validation, and the final two years (2013--2014) are held out as
a test set. The validation set is used only for early-stopping decisions, while
the test set is held out entirely during training and model selection and is
evaluated once, after training, for the reported $R^2$ scores. Crucially, all
normalization statistics are computed from the training years alone, so no
information from the validation or test period leaks into the model. This yields
45,136 training, 2,912 validation, and 2,912 test samples across the full
35-year record.

 Revised abstract (temporal split) -- ready to paste

Accurate representation of ocean--atmosphere surface fluxes is critical for
coupled climate simulations, but computing these fluxes with a full atmospheric
model is computationally expensive. We present MEMO (Memory-Enhanced Marine Flux
Output), a neural emulator that predicts five key surface fluxes---TAUX, TAUY,
SHFLX, LHFLX, and QFLX---directly from the ocean state, eliminating the need for
atmospheric model inference. MEMO is a five-level U-Net trained on 31 years of
6-hourly CESM2 output (1980--2010) and evaluated over ocean and sea-ice points on
two fully held-out future years (2013--2014), a temporal split that measures
genuine out-of-sample generalization rather than interpolation within the
training period. We find that providing 24 hours of ocean-state history is the
single most important design choice: relative to a memory-free baseline, it
improves held-out wind-stress skill from $R^2 = 0.377$ to $0.673$ for TAUX and
from $0.183$ to $0.571$ for TAUY, and raises the heat and moisture fluxes to high
accuracy (LHFLX: $0.898$, QFLX: $0.909$). Without memory the emulator overfits
the training years almost immediately and generalizes poorly across all five
fluxes. Wind stress remains the hardest target because the near-surface wind is
not an input; light regularization---spatial dropout and a physically exact
longitudinal augmentation---further recovers this skill, reaching $R^2 = 0.72$
(TAUX) and $0.62$ (TAUY). An auxiliary atmospheric CO$_2$ channel provides
negligible benefit once SST is known, even though the test years lie above the
training-period CO$_2$ range. These findings show that MEMO captures the dominant
mechanisms of ocean--atmosphere coupling and generalizes to unseen future
climate states, offering a promising route toward faster coupled climate
simulations.

%------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Network architecture}
%------------------------------------------------------------------
MEMO is a five-level encoder--decoder U-Net with a base width of 64 channels
(channel progression $64\to128\to128\to256\to512$). We use five resolution
levels rather than six because the $192\times288$ grid is divisible by $2^5$ but
not $2^6$, which avoids spatial padding at the bottleneck. To respect the
periodicity of the sphere, every convolution is preceded by \emph{circular}
padding in longitude and \emph{replicate} padding in latitude; this zonally
periodic boundary condition is also what makes the longitudinal data
augmentation (Section~\ref{sec:aug}) physically exact. Each encoder block applies
this padding followed by a stride-2 convolution (kernel size 4), batch
normalization, and a LeakyReLU ($\alpha=0.2$) activation; each decoder block
applies bilinear upsampling, the same padding, a convolution, batch
normalization, and a ReLU activation. Convolutional weights are initialized from
$\mathcal{N}(0, 0.02)$ and batch-normalization scales from $\mathcal{N}(1, 0.02)$.

%------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Training}
%------------------------------------------------------------------
We minimize the mean-squared error between predicted and target fluxes, averaged
over ocean/sea-ice points only. Models are optimized with AdamW (learning rate
$10^{-3}$, weight decay $10^{-4}$) using a cosine learning-rate schedule and a
batch size of 8. Training runs for up to 500 epochs with early stopping
(patience 40) on the validation loss, and the checkpoint with the lowest
validation loss is retained for evaluation. All experiments were run in PyTorch
on a single NVIDIA A100 GPU.

%------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Evaluation metric}
%------------------------------------------------------------------
We report the coefficient of determination $R^2$ separately for each target
flux. For a given variable, predictions and truth are pooled over all
ocean/sea-ice grid points and all test timesteps, and
\begin{equation}
  R^2 = 1 - \frac{\sum_i (y_i - \hat{y}_i)^2}{\sum_i (y_i - \bar{y})^2},
\end{equation}
where the sums run over the pooled spatiotemporal sample, $\hat{y}_i$ is the
prediction, and $\bar{y}$ is the mean of the truth over that sample. This pooled
spatiotemporal $R^2$ rewards skill in both the spatial pattern and the temporal
evolution of each flux.

%------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Generalization and regularization}\label{sec:aug}
%------------------------------------------------------------------
The temporal split evaluates extrapolation to unseen future years, which exposes
overfitting that a random split does not. To improve out-of-sample skill---
particularly for wind stress---we employ two regularizers. First, we apply
longitudinal data augmentation: during training only, each sample is circularly
shifted by a random offset along the longitude axis, with the inputs, targets,
and ocean mask rolled by the same amount so they remain aligned. This
transformation is exact rather than approximate because the grid is zonally
periodic and the network uses circular longitude padding. Second, we apply
spatial dropout ($p=0.1$) within the U-Net blocks. We assess each lever
independently---dropout, weight decay, longitudinal augmentation, network
capacity, and auxiliary CO$_2$ input---before combining the levers that improve
held-out test skill.

%------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{MEMO generalizes to unseen future years}
%------------------------------------------------------------------
MEMO is trained on CESM2 output from 1980--2010 and evaluated on two completely
held-out future years (2013--2014). This temporal split provides a stringent
test of out-of-sample generalization because the model must predict climate
states that occur after the training period.

The emulator predicts heat and moisture fluxes with high accuracy on these
unseen years. The best model achieves $R^2 = 0.848$ for sensible heat flux
(SHFLX), $0.905$ for latent heat flux (LHFLX), and $0.914$ for moisture flux
(QFLX). These results indicate that the relationship between ocean conditions
and heat or moisture exchange remains stable across time.

Wind stress is substantially more difficult to predict. The best model reaches
$R^2 = 0.718$ for TAUX and $0.624$ for TAUY. This lower skill is expected
because near-surface winds are not provided as inputs and must instead be
inferred indirectly from the ocean state.

%------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Temporal memory is the primary driver of performance}
%------------------------------------------------------------------
The largest performance gains come from providing the model with 24 hours of
ocean-state history. The memory-free baseline generalizes poorly to future
years, achieving only $R^2 = 0.377$ for TAUX and $0.183$ for TAUY. Adding
lagged SST and sea-ice fields increases these scores to $0.673$ and $0.571$,
respectively, while also substantially improving the heat and moisture fluxes.

These results suggest that information about the recent evolution of the ocean
is essential for predicting surface fluxes, particularly wind stress. Unlike
the heat and moisture fluxes, wind stress depends strongly on atmospheric
conditions that are not directly observed by the model. Temporal memory allows
the network to infer part of this missing information from recent changes in the
ocean state.

Regularization provides a smaller but measurable improvement. Adding spatial
dropout increases TAUX from $0.693$ to $0.716$ and TAUY from $0.592$ to $0.622$,
while longitudinal augmentation yields a further modest gain. In contrast,
increasing weight decay, reducing network capacity, or adding atmospheric
CO$_2$ as an input channel provides little or no improvement. Even though the
held-out years contain CO$_2$ concentrations outside the training range, the
ocean state already appears to contain the information required for accurate
flux prediction.

Overall, temporal memory is the dominant factor controlling performance,
whereas additional inputs and regularization contribute only incremental
improvements.
